The Legend of the Sword in the Stone

A Linked Open Data project exploring the Arthurian legend and its cultural heritage significance

Explore the Project

PROJECT

This project was developed for the Information Science and Cultural Heritage course at the University of Bologna, taught by Professor Francesca Tomasi and Professor Marilena Daquino.

This LODLAM project draws inspiration from the centuries-old Arthurian legend to collect, analyze, and semantically interlink a curated corpus of cultural heritage materials—including texts, manuscripts, artworks, historical documents, and audiovisual resources—related to the Arthurian cycle. These materials are sourced from libraries, archives, and museums (LAM), weaving a rich, cross-media network of digital objects.

By leveraging Linked Open Data (LOD) methodologies, the project constructs a dynamic knowledge graph, where entities are interconnected across disciplines and institutions. This framework not only enhances scholarly research but also enables innovative forms of digital storytelling, ensuring the Arthurian legacy—from medieval chronicles to modern film adaptations—is preserved, accessible, and perpetually reinterpreted.

STUDY OF THE DOMAIN

IDEA

"It is said that once upon a time,
England flourished with brave knights;
the good king died without an heir,
and so everyone coveted power.
Only a miracle could save
the kingdom from war and destruction:
it was the sword in the stone
that appeared one day".
- The Sword in the Stone, 1963

The topic chosen for this project is the legend of the sword in the stone, one of the foundational myths of Western tradition, rich in symbolic and narrative elements that have endured through the centuries. Its origins lie in Celtic traditions and the oral tales of ancient Britain, gradually evolving into a full-fledged saga thanks to medieval reinterpretations, especially starting with Historia Regum Britanniae by Geoffrey of Monmouth (12th century).

At the heart of the legend is the figure of King Arthur, a just and noble ruler, chosen according to tradition by drawing the mythical sword Excalibur, a symbol of divine legitimacy and power. At his side stands the wise and mysterious Merlin, enchanter and advisor, representing arcane knowledge and magical forces that guide and protect the king's destiny and that of his realm—the mythical Camelot.

The legend's popularity did not fade in the Middle Ages. The Arthurian cycle enjoyed vast literary success, from Chrétien de Troyes to Thomas Malory, followed by a 19th-century revival with Tennyson, and modern adaptations in fantasy novels, graphic novels, films, and television series. In cinema and TV, works such as Excalibur (1981), King Arthur (2004), and the Merlin series (BBC) have given new life to these characters, proving the continued relevance of the myth.
